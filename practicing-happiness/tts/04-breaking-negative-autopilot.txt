**Chapter Four. Breaking the Negative Autopilot.** [pause:800]

Before you can build what you want, you need to interrupt what has been running. [pause:600]
That is the precise purpose of this chapter. [pause:700]
Not the elimination of negative thinking. Not the pretence that difficult things are easy. [pause:600]
But the interruption of automatic, habitual negativity that runs regardless of what is actually happening. [pause:800]

Here is the story of Julien. [pause:500]

Julien grew up in a household where worry was the primary language of care. [pause:600]
His mother worried about the weather, about other drivers, about food safety, about minor health symptoms in herself and everyone around her. [pause:700]
This was not carelessness. This was how she expressed love — by anticipating what could go wrong and preparing for it comprehensively. [pause:700]
By the time Julien was a young adult, he had absorbed this orientation so completely it had become invisible to him. [pause:700]
His mind, given any idle moment, would begin scanning. [pause:500]
What is the thing that could go wrong here? [pause:400]
What is probably secretly worse than it looks? [pause:400]
What should I be worried about that I have not noticed yet? [pause:700]

This scanning did not feel like a habit. [pause:500]
It felt like thinking. [pause:500]
It felt like being appropriately careful. [pause:500]
It felt like him. [pause:800]

Julien spent most of his thirties with the quiet background experience of a person who is perpetually bracing for an impact that usually never comes. [pause:700]
Not dramatically suffering. Just constantly activated. [pause:600]
Just never quite at rest. [pause:700]

The moment that changed things was not a revelation. [pause:500]
It was a question a friend asked him one afternoon, almost casually. [pause:500]
Has the worrying ever actually prevented a bad thing from happening? Or has it just made the time before the bad thing more unpleasant? [pause:900]

Julien did not answer immediately. [pause:500]
He sat with that. [pause:600]
And for the first time, he began to separate the feeling of thinking from the usefulness of that thinking. [pause:800]
The worry felt like preparation. But it was not. [pause:600]
It was the habitual activation of a pattern his mother had given him, running automatically, regardless of whether it served anything. [pause:800]

That separation — between the habitual pattern and its usefulness — was the beginning of everything. [pause:900]

[silence:400]

The brain creates pathways through repetition the same way a forest creates trails. [pause:600]
The paths walked most frequently become wide, clear, and easy to take. [pause:600]
The paths walked rarely become overgrown and faint. [pause:600]
Julien had walked the worry-path so many times it had become a motorway. [pause:600]
A new path was possible. But it had to be walked to become real. [pause:800]

Here is the five-step interruption sequence. [pause:500]

Step one: Notice. [pause:400]
Catch the autopilot while it is running. [pause:400]
The moment you realise this pattern is happening right now, you have already stepped outside it. [pause:700]
You do not need to catch it immediately. Catching it three minutes after it began is still meaningful. [pause:600]
The practice is simply to catch it earlier over time. [pause:700]

Step two: Name it. [pause:400]
There is the worry loop. There is the catastrophising. There is the self-critical pattern. [pause:500]
Naming it creates distance. Distance creates choice. [pause:700]

Step three: Pause physically. [pause:400]
A single slow breath. A deliberate unclenching of the jaw. A moment of conscious stillness. [pause:600]
The body interruption breaks the neurological momentum of the pattern. [pause:700]

Step four: Redirect. [pause:400]
Introduce a more accurate, balanced, present-oriented thought. [pause:500]
Not optimistic denial. Not forced cheerfulness. [pause:400]
A thought that is genuinely true and genuinely more constructive than what the pattern just produced. [pause:700]
This will definitely fail becomes: I do not know how this will go. I can respond to what is actually happening. [pause:600]
Something is probably going wrong becomes: Let me look at what is actually in front of me. [pause:800]

Step five: Continue. [pause:400]
Return to what you were doing. [pause:400]
Do not spend time analysing the pattern or measuring your performance. [pause:400]
Simply have done the practice and moved on. [pause:800]
